The Tour de France

Introduction

The Tour de France is an annual men's multiple stage bicycle race primarily held in France. It is one of the most prestigious and difficult cycling races in the world, attracting top cyclists from around the globe.

History

The race was first organized in 1903 to increase the sales of the newspaper L'Auto. Since then, it has been held annually, except during the two World Wars. The Tour de France has grown significantly in scope and popularity over the years.

How It Works

The race consists of 21 day-long stages, over a 23-day period, covering approximately 3,500 kilometers (2,200 mi). The stages are varied, including flat stages, hilly stages, mountain stages, and individual time trials.

Cyclists compete as part of professional teams, each consisting of nine riders. The overall winner is determined by the lowest cumulative time across all stages, and they are awarded the famous yellow jersey.

Famous Winners

Some well-known winners of the Tour de France include:

  • Lance Armstrong (USA) - initially won 7 titles (1999-2005, but later stripped of titles)
  • Chris Froome (UK) - 4-time winner (2013, 2015, 2016, 2017)
  • Bernard Hinault (France) - 5-time winner (1978, 1979, 1981, 1982, 1985)
  • Eddy Merckx (Belgium) - 5-time winner (1969-1972, 1974)

Fun Facts

  • The Tour de France is often referred to as "Le Tour" or "La Grande Boucle".
  • The race was initially only open to French riders, but now it includes international competitors.
  • In addition to the yellow jersey, there are also jerseys for the best climber (polka dot jersey), best sprinter (green jersey), and best young rider (white jersey).
  • The route of the race changes every year, but it traditionally ends on the Champs-Élysées in Paris.
